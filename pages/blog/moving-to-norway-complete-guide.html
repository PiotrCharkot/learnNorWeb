Moving to Norway: The Complete Practical Guide for Newcomers (2026)

May 2026 • 15 min read • Updated with 2025–2026 data

Moving to Norway is one of the most rewarding — and logistically involved — decisions you can make. Whether you are coming for work, love, studies, or simply because you fell in love with the fjords, the process of actually settling in requires careful preparation across four parallel tracks: securing your legal right to stay, registering yourself with Norwegian authorities, finding housing, and getting your finances in order.

This guide walks you through all of it, step by step — from the moment you decide to move, through your first weeks in Norway, and into the longer-term process of building a life there. We have used official data from UDI, Skatteetaten, Statistics Norway (SSB), and other Norwegian authorities, updated for 2025–2026.

Step 1: Understanding Your Residence Basis

The first and most important question is not "which visa do I need?" — it is which legal basis gives you the right to live in Norway? The answer depends entirely on where you are from. There are three main paths:

Nordic Citizens (Sweden, Denmark, Finland, Iceland)

If you hold a Swedish, Danish, Finnish or Icelandic passport, you have the easiest path by far. You do not need a residence permit or any approval from UDI. You simply move to Norway and — if you intend to stay for six months or more — you notify the Folkeregisteret (the National Population Register). That's it.

EEA/EU Citizens

Citizens of EU and EEA countries have the right to live and work in Norway thanks to the freedom of movement within the European Economic Area. However, you are required to register with the Norwegian police if you plan to stay more than three months. If your stay will last six months or more, you must also register with Skatteetaten/Folkeregisteret.

You will need to document your basis for staying — typically proof of employment, self-employment, studies, or sufficient financial means if you are not working. Bring your passport or national ID card, proof of address, and relevant documents for work or studies.

💡 Tip: EEA citizens can use the Service Centre for Foreign Workers (Servicesenteret for utenlandske arbeidstakere) in Oslo, Bergen, Trondheim, or Stavanger. You register with police and Skatteetaten on the same visit, often the same day.

Non-EEA Citizens (All Other Countries)

If you are from outside the EEA — including Poland (note: Poland is in the EEA!), Ukraine, the Philippines, the USA, or any non-European country — the general rule is that you need a residence permit before you travel to Norway. You apply through UDI (the Directorate of Immigration), pay the relevant fee, submit documentation, and wait for a decision before booking flights.

⚠️ Important: Do not book non-refundable flights or give notice on your current home until you have received a positive permit decision from UDI. Processing times vary significantly by permit type, nationality, and document quality. Check UDI's website for current estimated times.

Step 2: Applying for a Permit (Non-EEA Residents)

The most common permit types for people moving to Norway from outside the EEA are:

Permit Type Who It Is For Key Requirements Fee (2026)
Skilled Worker Non-EEA citizens with a job offer in Norway Concrete job offer, documented qualifications 6,300 NOK
Student Non-EEA students accepted at Norwegian institutions Full-time study acceptance, proof of 166,859 NOK/year for living expenses 5,800 NOK
Family Immigration Partners, children, and certain relatives of Norwegian/Nordic/EEA residents Documented relationship; reference person typically needs income of at least 416,512 NOK/year pre-tax and housing 11,500 NOK

Each permit type has a dedicated application guide on UDI's website (udi.no). Applications are submitted online and followed up with biometrics at a VFS Global application centre in your country, or at a Norwegian embassy.

Once you receive your permit decision, you travel to Norway and then — within a few days of arrival — contact the police to register and collect your residence card (oppholdskort). This card is your physical proof of legal right to be in Norway.

Step 3: Registering After You Arrive

Once you land in Norway, a chain of practical registrations begins. The order matters.

For EEA Citizens:

  1. Book an appointment at the Service Centre for Foreign Workers (or the local police station)
  2. Register your right of residence with the police (takes about 30 minutes)
  3. If staying 6+ months, register with Folkeregisteret at Skatteetaten (often done the same day)
  4. Apply for your skattekort (tax deduction card) as soon as you start working

For Non-EEA Citizens (with a permit):

  1. Book an appointment with the police via UDI's website immediately after arrival
  2. Attend the appointment with passport, permit decision letter, and biometrics
  3. Receive your residence card (oppholdskort)
  4. If staying 9+ months on a study permit, you will normally be registered as a resident and receive a fødselsnummer
  5. Apply for skattekort when starting work

Step 4: Your Norwegian ID Number

In Norway, everything is tied to your personal ID number. Without one, opening a bank account, using digital government services, seeing a doctor, or even getting paid properly becomes significantly more difficult. There are two types:

Fødselsnummer (National Identity Number)

This is the full Norwegian personal number — 11 digits — assigned when you are registered as a resident in Norway (registered in Folkeregisteret). It is the gold standard for participating in Norwegian society. With a fødselsnummer you get full access to digital services (BankID, Altinn, Helsenorge, DigiPost), can open a bank account, sign leases digitally, and use public services seamlessly.

D-Number

If you are in Norway but have not yet been registered as a resident, you may be issued a D-number — a temporary identification number used for tax, banking, and employment while you are working towards full resident status. You cannot apply for a D-number yourself; it is requested on your behalf by a public authority, employer, or bank that needs to identify you.

💡 30-Day Rule: If you were issued a D-number and later become a full resident of Norway within 30 days of the D-number being issued, you can report your move without a new ID check. After 30 days, a full ID check is required again.

Step 5: Finding Housing in Norway

Housing is consistently one of the biggest practical challenges for newcomers to Norway — both finding a place and affording it. The general, well-tested advice from housing experts and expats alike is: rent first, buy later.

This is not just about money. In the early months, you often lack the Norwegian income history, banking relationship, BankID, and local knowledge needed to buy wisely. You also may not yet know which neighbourhood, commute route, or city actually fits your life. Renting for the first year — or even two — gives you the freedom to learn before making an irreversible decision.

If You Do Want to Buy

Norway's housing market is competitive, especially in Oslo. In 2024, the government lowered the required down payment for a mortgage from 15% to 10% of the purchase price. However, banks must still stay within a maximum loan-to-value ratio of 90%, and lending regulations are tightly enforced by Finanstilsynet. For newcomers without established Norwegian income and credit history, mortgage approval is difficult.

Where to Search for Rentals

The main platforms for finding rental accommodation in Norway are:

  • Finn.no/eiendom — the largest Norwegian property portal for both sale and rental
  • Hybel.no — focused on rooms, shared flats, and student accommodation
  • Facebook groups — many expat communities and city-specific groups have rental listings
  • Employer or university housing — some companies and all major universities provide temporary housing for new arrivals

Some landlords quote a rent that includes electricity and heating (strøm og oppvarming inkludert), while others charge those separately. Monthly utilities can add 500–2,000 NOK to your real costs depending on the season and how energy-efficient the building is.

Step 7: Lease Contracts, Deposits & Your Rights

Norwegian tenancy law is strongly protective of tenants — but only if you know your rights and use them. Here is what every newcomer needs to know.

Always Use a Written Contract

Verbal agreements are legally binding in Norway, but extremely hard to enforce. Always insist on a written contract — ideally using the standard contract from Forbrukerrådet (the Norwegian Consumer Council), which is free to download and commonly used. This contract explicitly states that the Tenancy Act (husleieloven) supersedes any agreement that is worse for the tenant.

At the time of moving in, walk through the apartment together with the landlord and document any existing damage or wear — with photos and written notes. This protects both parties at the end of the tenancy.

Deposit Rules

The deposit (depositum) rules in Norway are strict and often misunderstood by newcomers:

  • The deposit must be held in a locked deposit account (sperret depositumskonto) in the tenant's name, not the landlord's
  • The landlord must pay the cost of opening the deposit account
  • The maximum deposit is six months' rent
  • Paying deposit directly into the landlord's private account is illegal under Norwegian law
⚠️ Watch out for rental scams! A common scam targeting newcomers is a fake listing where the "landlord" asks for a deposit before you have seen the apartment, or asks you to wire money abroad. If it feels off, it probably is. Always meet the landlord in person, see the property, and use a proper deposit account.

What Can the Landlord Charge For?

Norwegian tenancy law limits what landlords can charge in addition to the base rent. Tenants can be asked to pay separately for actual consumption-based costs like electricity, heating, and water/drainage. However, landlords cannot charge additional line items for things like hallway cleaning (trappevask), cable TV, or internet as separate fees on top of rent unless those were already baked into the rent amount. If you want internet, you typically arrange it yourself.

If you have a dispute with your landlord, Norway has the Husleietvistutvalget (HTU) — a low-cost mediation body specifically for rental disputes. It is faster and cheaper than going to court, and widely used.

Step 8: Opening a Bank Account

A Norwegian bank account is essential for receiving your salary, paying rent, and eventually getting BankID — Norway's universal digital identity system used for signing contracts, logging into government services, and much more.

Most banks require a fødselsnummer or D-number, along with your passport or ID card and documentation of your right to stay in Norway. Common options for newcomers include:

  • DNB — Norway's largest bank, wide branch network, good English support
  • Nordea — another large Norwegian-Scandinavian bank
  • Sbanken / SpareBank 1 — user-friendly digital banks
  • Revolut or Wise — international digital banks useful for managing money before you get a Norwegian account
💡 BankID: Once your bank account is active, apply for BankID as soon as possible. This digital ID is used for everything in Norway — from signing your lease to booking a GP appointment to filing taxes. Life in Norway without BankID is much more complicated.

Step 9: Tax Card and Skatteetaten

As soon as you start working in Norway, your employer needs your skattekort (tax deduction card). This is a digital card from Skatteetaten (the Norwegian Tax Administration) that tells your employer how much income tax to withhold from your salary each month.

Without a skattekort, your employer is required to deduct 50% tax by default — which is painful and takes time to reclaim. Apply for your skattekort as soon as you have a D-number or fødselsnummer. You can apply online at skatteetaten.no or in person at any tax office.

Norway uses a self-assessment tax system. Each spring, Skatteetaten will send you a pre-filled tax return (skattemelding) based on reported income. You review it, correct anything that is wrong, and either receive a refund or pay any outstanding tax. Most newcomers with a single employer and straightforward income will find Norwegian taxes simple to handle.

Key tax authority contacts:

  • Skatteetaten — tax registration, tax return, D-number (skatteetaten.no)
  • NAV — employment, social security, sick leave benefits, parental leave (nav.no)
  • Arbeidstilsynet — workers' rights, minimum wages, conditions of employment (arbeidstilsynet.no)

Step 10: Healthcare and the Fastlege System

Norway has an excellent public healthcare system, but it is structured differently from many countries. The cornerstone is the fastlege — your assigned GP (general practitioner). Every registered resident in Norway is entitled to a fastlege, and they are your first point of contact for almost all non-emergency health needs.

Once you have a fødselsnummer, register with a fastlege through helsenorge.no. Some GPs have long waiting lists, so register as soon as possible. In the meantime, if you have urgent but non-life-threatening medical needs, you can use a legevakt (emergency out-of-hours clinic) in your area.

For more serious or specialist care, your fastlege refers you to the relevant hospital or specialist. Emergency services (ambulance: 113, emergency room: 112) are always available regardless of your registration status.

Step 11: Learning Norwegian — Why It Matters and How to Start

Many newcomers to Norway discover that most Norwegians speak excellent English — and use it freely when they detect a foreign accent. This is helpful in the short term, but it is also a trap: it makes it very easy to go months or years without really learning Norwegian, and this limits your social life, career opportunities, and sense of belonging.

Norwegian is actually one of the easier languages for English speakers to learn. It shares a large amount of vocabulary with English, has relatively simple grammar (no cases, no gendered adjective agreement, moderate verb conjugation), and the pronunciation — while different — follows predictable rules once you know them.

Why you should start now, not later:

  • Many jobs in Norway — especially outside Oslo and in public-facing roles — require functional Norwegian
  • Government services, official letters, and bureaucracy are primarily in Norwegian
  • Norwegian fluency is required for permanent residence and citizenship
  • Socially, speaking Norwegian — even imperfectly — opens doors that English cannot

Practical ways to get started:

  • Take the free Norwegian language classes available through your municipality (kommunen) — many municipalities are legally required to offer these to newcomers
  • Use a structured language app that works in your native language (not just in English)
  • Listen to Norwegian radio (NRK P1) and podcasts — even when you understand very little, the exposure helps
  • Find a language exchange partner or conversation group
  • Use flashcards to build vocabulary systematically — frequency-first, not alphabet-first

Your Moving-to-Norway Checklist

Use this as a quick reference to track your progress through the key steps:

Before You Move:

  • ☐ Confirm your legal basis for staying (Nordic / EEA / non-EEA permit)
  • ☐ Apply for UDI permit if required — and wait for a positive decision before booking flights
  • ☐ Secure at least temporary housing before arrival (Finn.no, university housing, employer housing)
  • ☐ Gather all documents: passport, employment/study contract, proof of finances
  • ☐ Research your destination city: commutes, neighbourhoods, cost of living
  • ☐ Start learning basic Norwegian phrases

First Two Weeks in Norway:

  • ☐ Register with the police (EEA) or collect your residence card (non-EEA)
  • ☐ Register with Folkeregisteret if staying 6+ months
  • ☐ Apply for fødselsnummer or D-number
  • ☐ Apply for skattekort as soon as you have an ID number and an employer
  • ☐ Open a Norwegian bank account
  • ☐ Apply for BankID
  • ☐ Register with a fastlege (GP) at helsenorge.no

First Three Months:

  • ☐ Sign a proper rental lease with a locked deposit account
  • ☐ Register for municipal Norwegian language classes if applicable
  • ☐ Enrol children in barnehage (kindergarten) or school if relevant
  • ☐ Connect utilities (electricity: usually Tibber, Fjordkraft or similar)
  • ☐ Arrange contents insurance (innboforsikring)
  • ☐ Review your first skattemelding (tax return) the following spring

This article is for informational purposes only. Immigration rules, fees, and requirements change regularly. Always verify the latest information directly with UDI, Skatteetaten, and other official Norwegian authorities before making decisions.
